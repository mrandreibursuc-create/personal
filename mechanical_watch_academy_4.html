Chapter 01 · Power

Everything begins with a wound spring

A mechanical watch stores all of its energy in one part: the mainspring, a long ribbon of hardened alloy coiled inside a drum called the barrel. Winding the crown twists the spring tighter around a central shaft, the arbor. As the spring fights to unwind, it turns the barrel — and the barrel drives everything else in the watch.

Use the slider below to wind the spring and watch its coils bunch around the arbor. The torque curve on the right shows a hidden problem: a spring pushes hardest when fully wound and weakest when nearly flat. Good movements are designed to run in the middle of that curve, which is one reason a watch keeps better time when worn and wound daily.

Power reserve 6 hTorque low
Release the arbor with nothing attached and the spring dumps ~40 hours of stored energy in about two seconds.

That release button demonstrates the core problem of watchmaking. Left alone, a mainspring doesn't meter its energy — it explodes it. A hand attached directly to the barrel would spin wildly for a moment, then stop forever. The next three chapters build the machinery that turns this violent unwinding into one quiet tick every fraction of a second, for forty hours straight.

Real mainsprings hold enough energy to injure you. During a service, the spring is let down before anything else: hold the crown, pull the click away with a peg, and let the crown slip backward through your fingers, a half-turn at a time. Never open a barrel that hasn't been let down.
Go deeper — spring metallurgy, the fusee, and where "40 hours" comes from

Why thickness rules everything. A mainspring's torque scales with its width and with the cube of its thickness (M ∝ E·w·t³/L). A spring 10% thicker is a third stronger — which is why "just fit a stronger spring" is a dangerous cure: it can push amplitude into knocking territory (Chapter 13) and chew the winding work. Modern springs are cold-rolled Nivaflex-type alloys: essentially unbreakable and "non-setting", unlike blued carbon-steel springs in vintage watches, which take a permanent set and deliver a sagging torque curve even when intact — a routine replacement item in any vintage service.

The old solution to the torque curve: the fusee. Before going barrels, watches wound a chain onto a cone. Fully wound, the chain pulled on the cone's small radius; as the spring weakened it worked on a larger radius — a continuously variable transmission delivering near-constant torque, in 1450. It was abandoned because the S-curved slipping mainspring plus a well-designed escapement made 200 parts of chain unnecessary.

Do the reserve math on our own movement. Our barrel turns once per 6.4 hours (Chapter 03) and a healthy spring gives about 6¼ usable turns: 6.25 × 6.4 ≈ 40 hours. Every power-reserve number on a spec sheet is exactly this multiplication — usable barrel turns × hours per barrel revolution. Long-reserve calibers get there with thinner, longer springs (more turns), stacked double barrels, or slower trains.

Go deeper — barrel engineering: fitting a mainspring by the numbers

Choosing a replacement mainspring is arithmetic, not luck. Wound on the arbor, the spring's coils occupy an area L·e (length × thickness); relaxed, the same area presses against the wall. The turns you can actually extract are what's left between those two states — and the classic result is the half-area rule: maximum turns when the spring fills exactly half the free space between arbor and wall. Too long a spring chokes its own barrel; too short wastes the volume. Play spring-fitter — barrel Ø 10.4 mm, arbor Ø 3.4 mm:

Thickness is the trump card (torque ∝ t³): going 0.005 mm thicker to "fix" low amplitude adds ~15% torque — often straight into knocking.
Chapter 02 · Gears

Trading force for speed

Over a full wind, the barrel turns only about 7 times — but the second hand must turn about 2,400 times. Gears make that exchange. When a large driving wheel meshes with a small pinion, both move tooth-by-tooth together, so the pinion — with fewer teeth — must complete far more revolutions.

Change the tooth counts below and watch the marked dots. The ratio of teeth is the ratio of speeds — and, just as importantly, the inverse ratio of torque. The pinion spins faster but pushes weaker. Watchmaking spends the mainspring's brute force to buy speed and gentleness.

A single pair can't reach 343:1 in a watch-sized case — the big wheel would need to be dinner-plate sized or the pinion microscopic. The answer is to chain modest ratios: 8 × 7.5 × 6.4 ≈ 384. That chain is the going train.
Chapter 03 · The going train

Four wheels between the spring and the tick

The barrel drives the center wheel (one turn per hour — the minute hand rides here), which drives the third wheel, which drives the fourth wheel (one turn per minute — the second hand), which finally drives the escape wheel. Each stage is a big wheel driving the next axis's small pinion, multiplying speed at every step.

Barrel 1 rev / 6.4 hEscape wheel 1 rev / 6 s
Spoked wheels aren't decoration: cutting material out reduces rotational inertia, so the train can stop and restart 8 times per second without strain.

Notice what we still don't have: anything that sets the pace. This train faithfully multiplies whatever speed the barrel provides — and the barrel would still love to unwind all at once. The pace-setter comes next, and it's the most beautiful mechanism in all of horology.

Chapter 04 · The escapement

The Swiss lever, frame by frame

The escapement is a gatekeeper. The escape wheel — the last wheel of the train — is locked by one of two ruby pallet stones mounted on a pivoting pallet fork. Each swing of the balance flicks the fork, letting the wheel escape by exactly one half-tooth before the opposite stone catches it. That capture is the tick you hear.

But the escapement does a second, subtler job: as each tooth slides across the angled face of a pallet stone, it pushes back, sending a pulse of energy through the fork into the balance. The watch pays the balance a tiny wage for every beat, which keeps it swinging forever. Scrub the timeline to watch a single beat in slow motion:

Phases: lock → unlock (roller jewel knocks the fork) → impulse (wheel pushes the fork, fork pushes the balance) → drop → lock on the other stone.
The pallet stones and the roller jewel are synthetic ruby — the "17 jewels" on a dial are bearings and contact surfaces, not ornament. Ruby is nearly as hard as diamond and glides on steel with very little friction, so these parts survive 250 million impacts a year.
Under a loupe, a healthy escapement shows a tiny safety margin called drop between tooth and stone, and the fork should snap crisply against its banking pins. A fork that creeps or hesitates means a bent pivot, oil in the wrong place, or magnetism.
Go deeper — lock, drop, draw: the five adjustments, and the rival escapements

Escapement fitters live by five words. Lock: how deep the tooth lands on the stone's locking face (~1.5° — safety). Drop: the tooth's brief free flight between one stone releasing and the other catching (clearance for manufacturing error — but pure wasted energy). Slide: extra travel after locking as draw pulls the fork home to the banking. Draw: the locking faces are angled so that train torque actively pulls the fork against its banking pin — this is what holds the fork safely parked between beats and keeps the guard pin off the safety roller. Lift (impulse) angle: the total balance arc during which fork and jewel are engaged, typically 50–54° — the number you must type into a timegrapher.

On real movements lock is set by moving the pallet stones in their shellac seats with a heated tool; drop is fixed by the parts and checked, not adjusted.

The rivals. The cylinder escapement (Breguet's era) rested the tooth directly on the balance staff's hollow cylinder — simple, but constant friction on the balance meant mediocre timekeeping and fast wear. The detent (chronometer) escapement gives the balance one direct, almost frictionless impulse per oscillation with no lubrication needed at the impulse — supreme on a gimballed marine chronometer, hopeless on a wrist, where a shock mid-arc can trip it ("setting"). The co-axial (George Daniels, industrialized by Omega) splits locking and impulse across stacked wheels so impulses are pushing, not sliding — less friction-dependence and longer service intervals, at the price of demanding manufacture. The Swiss lever won the wrist because of one property the others lack: detached safety — the balance swings free 97% of the time, and the fork is actively held out of its way by draw.

Chapter 05 · The balance

The heartbeat: a wheel on a hairspring

What decides when the fork flips? The balance wheel, a weighted ring on the thinnest spring in the watch — the hairspring. Twist it and let go, and it oscillates with a natural period that depends on just two things: the spring's stiffness k and the wheel's rotational inertia I. The period is T = 2π√(I∕k) — and crucially, it barely depends on how hard you twist it. Big swing or small, each beat takes the same time. That property, called isochronism, is why a spring-and-wheel can keep time.

Tune the oscillator below. Give it a flick, then change stiffness and inertia and watch the beat rate respond:

Period —Rate —
Stiffer spring → faster beat. Heavier rim → slower beat. Every regulating device in horology is a way of nudging k or I.

A real balance beats fast — typically 4 Hz, or 28,800 beats per hour — because a fast oscillator averages out disturbances like wrist motion. And it never gets to swing freely for long: twice per cycle, its impulse jewel slaps the pallet fork, unlocking the escape wheel and collecting its little payment of energy in return. Balance and escapement together form a closed loop: the balance times the escapement, and the escapement feeds the balance.

Go deeper — overcoils, free-sprung balances, temperature, and the hi-beat wars

The Breguet overcoil. A flat hairspring doesn't breathe concentrically — its center of mass shuttles sideways each oscillation, loading the pivots and splitting the vertical rates. Breguet's fix: raise the last coil and sweep it back over the spring in a calculated terminal curve. The spring then breathes like a lung, concentric, and isochronism across positions improves markedly. The cost is height and hand-finishing, which is why overcoils live in chronometry-focused calibers.

Free-sprung balances. The regulator's pins are themselves an isochronism defect: the spring bounces between them differently at different amplitudes. High-end solutions (Patek Gyromax, Rolex Microstella) delete the regulator entirely and tune the rate by turning tiny inertia weights on the balance rim — adjusting I instead of k, in the language of this chapter. More stable, harder to service: rate changes now need a key on the balance itself.

Temperature. Steel springs soften when warm — a plain steel hairspring loses roughly 11 seconds per day per °C. A century of metallurgy fixed this: first bimetallic cut balances that curled to compensate, then self-compensating alloys (Elinvar, then Nivarox), and now silicon, which is also completely non-magnetic. Slide the thermometer:

Steel Nivarox Silicon

Beat-rate history. 18,000 vph ruled for two centuries; the 1960s chased stability with 21,600, 28,800, then Zenith's and Seiko's 36,000 vph "hi-beats". Faster resists disturbance better — but doubles the escapement mileage and demands oils that survive it. 28,800 became the industry's truce between chronometry and service life.

Chapter 06 · Winding & the click

Why the crown ratchets

One problem left over from Chapter 01: the moment you let go of the crown, the mainspring would happily unwind backward through it. The fix is a mechanism so satisfying it named itself — the click. A sprung pawl rides on the teeth of the ratchet wheel screwed to the arbor. Wind forward and the click hops from tooth to tooth (that's the sound). Try to go backward, and it jams solid.

Wind forward to hear the click take up.
This is also why over-winding is mostly a myth in modern watches: manual movements simply stop firm at full wind, and automatics have a slipping bridle inside the barrel that lets the spring skid instead of breaking. A "wound too tight" watch that won't run is almost always a dirty watch — old oil has turned to glue.
Chapter 07 · Keyless works

The mechanism under your crown

Early watches were wound with a separate key; the "keyless works" is everything that lets one crown do two jobs. The stem carries a square section on which the sliding clutch (castle wheel) can slide but not spin freely. Pulling the crown moves the setting lever, whose finger rides a groove in the stem; the lever throws the yoke, which slides the clutch between two sets of teeth: pushed in, it drives the winding pinion toward the mainspring; pulled out, it drives the setting wheels toward the hands.

Position 1: the clutch's left (Breguet) teeth mesh with the winding pinion — turning the crown winds the barrel.
The keyless works is the most common repair a beginner meets: crowns get yanked, stems snap at the case tube, setting-lever springs break. Learn to release the stem (usually a dimple or the setting-lever screw next to it — press gently with the crown pushed in, or you can eject the setting lever and face a full teardown). A replacement stem is cut to length with a jeweler's saw and finished with a stone.
Chapter 08 · Motion works

Three hands from one train

The going train already contains a wheel turning once a minute (fourth wheel → second hand) and once an hour (center wheel → minute hand via the cannon pinion). The hour hand needs one more 12:1 reduction, handled by two small gears — the minute wheel and hour wheel — stacked around the same central axis. That stack of coaxial pipes is why all three hands can share one center.

The cannon pinion hides one more trick: it grips its shaft by friction alone. That slip-fit is what lets you set the time — turning the crown overpowers the friction and rotates the hands without fighting the whole gear train.

In setting mode, drag the slider to move the hands directly — the train stays frozen, only the friction-fit pinion turns.
Chapter 09 · Automatic winding

A pendulum on your wrist

An automatic adds a weighted rotor that swings with every movement of your arm. Its rotation passes through a pair of reverser wheels — tiny ratcheting gears that rectify motion so that both directions of rotor swing wind the mainspring the same way. Because the wearer never stops winding, automatic barrels contain a slipping bridle: past full wind, the mainspring's outer end simply skids along the greased barrel wall instead of breaking.

Reserve —
A day of desk work barely keeps a watch topped up; a walk winds it fully. Rotor bearings and reverser wheels are the classic wear points of automatic calibers.
Diagnostic tell: if hand-winding runs the watch fine but wearing it doesn't keep it alive, suspect the automatic module — worn rotor bearing (rotor wobbles and scrapes the caseback, leaving a shiny ring) or failed reversers (rotor spins freely both ways, ratchet never turns). On many calibers reversers are replaced, never oiled — ordinary oil makes them slip.
Go deeper — magic levers, micro-rotors, and the bumper era

Seiko's magic lever replaces the delicate Swiss reverser wheels with one eccentric pin driving two spring pawls — one pulls, one pushes, both rotor directions wind, and there are about four parts to break instead of forty. It's the reason a neglected Seiko 7S26 winds happily after decades in a drawer.

Micro-rotors sink a small heavy (platinum, tungsten) rotor into the movement plane for slimness — Piaget and Patek territory — trading winding efficiency for elegance. Peripheral rotors run a weighted ring around the movement's rim, keeping the view of the caliber unobstructed. And before full rotors were patented-around, 1940s–50s bumper automatics swung through ~120° and bounced off buffer springs — you can feel the thump on the wrist; collectors love them, and their buffer springs are a known service item.

Bench note on unwinding automatics: the sliding bridle means you can never fully "let down" an automatic through the crown — the last portion slips. And when re-greasing an automatic barrel wall, the braking grease (e.g., 8213) is applied in a few spots only: too much and the bridle slips early (poor reserve), too little and it knocks at full wind.

Chapter 10 · Shock protection & hairspring health

Protecting the two most fragile parts

The balance staff's pivots are thinner than a human hair — around 0.10 mm — and before the 1930s a dropped watch usually meant a snapped pivot. Shock protection (Incabloc is the famous name) mounts the balance jewels in a spring-loaded setting: a chaton holding the hole jewel and cap jewel, retained by a lyre spring. Under shock, the whole jewel setting moves and the sturdy shoulder of the staff takes the blow instead of its fragile tip.

1 m onto a wooden floor ≈ 5,000 g at the pivot.

Reading a sick hairspring

The hairspring must breathe concentrically, in one flat plane, with every coil clear of its neighbors. Anything else shows up instantly on the timegrapher. Learn the four states:

Coils open and close evenly around the collet — the spring "breathes". This is what you want to see.
Hairspring truing is the highest art in repair: two pairs of tweezers, working at the collet or stud, never mid-coil. But magnetism needs no skill to fix — a €20 demagnetizer and two seconds. It should always be your first test on a wildly fast watch: hold a compass near the movement, or just demagnetize on principle.
Chapter 11 · Regulation

Making it keep your time

A freshly assembled watch never runs perfectly. Two adjustments live on the balance bridge. The regulator is a lever carrying two pins that straddle the hairspring's outer coil: sliding it changes the spring's effective length, and a shorter spring is a stiffer spring — the watch runs faster. The stud carrier rotates the spring's fixed end to center the balance's resting position, correcting beat error — the asymmetry between tick and tock.

You're the watchmaker now. The target is 0.0 s/day and a beat error under 0.3 ms:

Rate —Beat error —
Real regulator scales are marked A/R (avance/retard) or +/−. Moves are tiny: a visible nudge of the lever is worth tens of seconds per day.
Go deeper — Etachron, timing washers, and why beat comes first

The Etachron system, on most modern ETA/Sellita movements, mounts the stud in a rotatable holder and the regulator pins in a slotted rotatable boot. It gives three tools: rotate the stud carrier for beat, slide the regulator for rate, and rotate the pin boot to open or close the pin gap — the gap itself changes isochronism (tight pins: amplitude-insensitive but positionally worse; open pins: the opposite). Turning the pin boot with a wound balance in place is delicate work; many hairsprings have died there.

Mean-time screws and washers. On screwed balances, opposite pairs of mean-time screws turn out symmetrically to slow (adds I) without disturbing poise; timing washers slipped under a screw do the same in coarser steps. Rule of thumb kept in every drawer: washers are chosen in pairs, always opposite, or you've just created Chapter 12's poise error yourself.

Why beat before rate: moving the stud rotates the whole spring, which drags the coil's position between the regulator pins and shifts effective length — so a beat correction changes the rate, but a rate correction barely touches beat. Fix the asymmetry first, then chase seconds.

Chapter 12 · Positional timing & poise

Why the same watch keeps six different times

Lay a watch dial-up and gravity presses along the balance staff; hang it crown-down and gravity pulls across the pivots. Friction changes, amplitude drops 30–50°, and any spot on the balance rim that is a few micrograms heavy — a poise error — starts stealing or adding time depending on where it hangs. Chronometer testing exists because of this: a certified movement must agree with itself across positions within a few seconds a day.

Here is the deep expert fact: a poise error's sign flips with amplitude. Below roughly 220° of swing, heavy-spot-down positions run slow; above 220°, the same error makes them run fast. Turn the watch, move the heavy spot, and change amplitude to see it:

This position —Δ across 6 positions —
Watchmakers exploit the sign flip: check poise at full wind (high amplitude) and again 24 h later (low amplitude). If a vertical position flips from fast to slow, the balance is out of poise — material is removed from the heavy spot, or timing washers are added opposite it.
Go deeper — what COSC actually tests

The chronometer certificate is a 15-day gauntlet: rates recorded daily in five positions (dial up, dial down, crown left, crown up, crown down) and at three temperatures (8, 23, 38 °C). The headline spec is a mean daily rate of −4/+6 s/d, but the harder numbers are the consistency ones: mean variation ≤ 2 s/d, maximum positional difference ≤ 10 s/d (that's the delta you computed above), and a thermal coefficient ≤ ±0.6 s/d/°C — Chapter 05's metallurgy, examined. A watch that averages +1 s/d can still fail on delta; chronometry is about agreement with yourself, not a single lucky rate. Métas (Master Chronometer) piles magnetism on top: full rate spec maintained after 15,000 gauss.

Chapter 13 · The timegrapher

Reading a watch's ECG

A timegrapher listens to the escapement through a microphone and plots every beat as a dot. A healthy watch draws two overlapping horizontal lines. Everything wrong with a movement has a signature in that trace — this machine is the single most important diagnostic tool on a repair bench.

Slope = rate error (uphill runs fast, downhill runs slow). Two separated lines = beat error. Scatter = low amplitude or dirt. Periodic waves = a bent pivot or damaged tooth somewhere in the train. Play with the controls, then test yourself with the fault presets:

Go deeper — how the machine computes amplitude, and the knocking failure

Each beat is not one sound but three: the unlock click, the impulse scrape, and the loud drop-lock. The machine times the gap Δt between the first and last — the time the balance spends crossing the lift angle. At mid-swing the balance's speed is proportional to its amplitude, so a shorter Δt means a bigger swing: A = lift ∕ (2·sin(π·f·Δt)). This is why the lift angle setting matters: feed it 52° when the caliber is 49° and every amplitude it prints is confidently wrong.

Listen for it on a quiet bench: a healthy watch's tick is a tight triplet.

Knocking (rebanking). Past ~330° of amplitude, the impulse jewel comes all the way around and strikes the outside of the fork horns. The tick turns into a gallop, the watch gains wildly, and the trace goes chaotic with an over-range amplitude reading. Causes: a too-strong replacement mainspring, or a freshly serviced, too-slippery escapement. Chapter 14 · Service

The anatomy of an overhaul

Everything above is why a mechanical watch needs periodic service: oils age, amplitude falls, wear begins. A full service follows a strict order — largely the reverse of how this course built the movement. The sequence below is the classic routine for a simple hand-wound caliber:

Let down the mainspring

Release all stored power via the click before touching anything. The single most important safety step.

Remove hands, dial, and motion works

Hand levers with dial protection; cannon pinion, minute wheel, hour wheel off the front.

Remove the balance assembly

The most fragile part comes off early and rests safely on the bench, hairspring untouched.

Strip the train: pallet fork, bridges, wheels, barrel

Photograph everything. Screws sorted by bridge — they are not interchangeable.

Clean

Ultrasonic or cleaning machine, multiple rinses. Shellac-set jewels (pallet stones, roller jewel) get gentler treatment.

Inspect under magnification

Pivots for wear, jewels for cracks, teeth for damage, hairspring for true. Replace, don't hope.

Reassemble and oil — sparingly

Rebuild in reverse. Lubrication is placed by the jewel cup, not flooded.

Train jewels: thin synthetic oil (9010) · Barrel & high-torque: grease (HP-1300 / 8200) · Pallet faces: 9415 · Keyless works: grease

Regulate on the timegrapher

Set beat error first, then rate — in several positions (dial up, dial down, crown down). Chapter 09 is the final exam of every service.

Go deeper — epilame, end-shake, and hand height

Epilame (Fixodrop) is an invisible anti-spread coating applied before oiling to parts where oil must stay put against centrifugal force and capillary creep: escape wheel teeth, pallet stones, cap jewels, reverser wheels. The classic co-axial-era rule: treat, run dry for a few minutes to scuff a track, then oil — the lubricant then sits in the scuffed channel like water in a groove. Skipping epilame on the escape wheel is why some services come back in a year with the amplitude down 40°.

End-shake and side-shake. Every arbor needs measurable but tiny axial play (end-shake, ~0.02–0.05 mm — lift each wheel gently with tweezers and watch for a just-visible tick of movement) and radial play in its jewel (side-shake). Too little: friction, stops when warm. Too much: depthing errors, wobble, that Chapter 13 wavy trace. Adjustment is a jeweling-tool job — pressing jewels micrometrically deeper or shallower — and it separates parts-swappers from watchmakers.

Hand-fitting heights cause more comebacks than escapements: the hour hand must clear the dial, the minute hand the hour hand, the second hand everything — with a case-back's worth of clearance to the crystal. Check with the movement dial-down and the watch turned in every position before casing: a hand that only touches crown-down will stop the watch on the owner's wrist and never on your timegrapher.

Go deeper — identifying calibers, lignes, and the Jacot tool

Every repair starts with identification. The caliber number hides under the balance or the dial on the mainplate (Swiss ébauches), or on the rotor/bridges. Movement size is quoted in lignes (‴): one ligne = 2.256 mm, so the classic 11½‴ men's movement is ~25.6 mm. With caliber and size you enter the parts world: bestfit/Ronda interchange books map thousands of vintage calibers to shared staffs and stems, and suppliers (Cousins UK, Jules Borel, Boley) list by caliber. Rule of the trade: identify before you disassemble — the photo of the markings costs nothing, the wrong mainspring costs a week.

The Jacot tool is where worn pivots go to be reborn: a miniature lathe bed with hardened, grooved runners. The pivot spins in a matching groove while a carbide burnisher work-hardens and polishes it a few microns smaller — restoring a scored pivot's mirror finish without replacing the staff. Burnishing, not cutting: the steel is compressed smooth, which actually hardens the surface. Together with the staking set (driving riveted staffs in and out) and the jeweling tool (Chapter 14's end-shake adjustments), it completes the trinity that separates restoration from parts-swapping — and all three tools outlive their owners.

Chapter 15 · Complications

Everything beyond the time

A complication is any function beyond hours, minutes and seconds. Almost all of them are geared off the motion works you already understand — the art is in switching, snapping, and remembering. The most universal complication is the date, and it hides a beautiful mechanism: the jumper.

The date ring can't simply be geared to the hour wheel — it would creep under the window all day, unreadable. Instead a date finger (one turn per 24 h) slowly loads the ring against a sprung jumper; around midnight the jumper wins and snaps the ring one full tooth in a fraction of a second. This is also why "never set the date between ~21:00 and 03:00" is real advice: in that window the finger is engaged with the ring — forcing the quickset can snap the finger or the jumper.

The rest of the family, in one tour

Chronograph. A second, independent gear train for the stopwatch hands, coupled to the going train on demand. Cheap movements switch it with a stamped cam; fine ones use a column wheel — a tiny castle turret whose pillars lift the levers, giving that crisp, light pusher feel. The coupling itself is either horizontal (two toothed wheels swing into mesh — simple, but the chrono hand can stutter on start) or a vertical clutch (friction plates, like a car — buttery start, can run permanently).

Moonphase. A disc with two moons behind a window, driven one tooth per day on a 59-tooth wheel: two lunar months of 29.5 days. Since a lunation is really 29.53 days, the display drifts one day every ~2.7 years — a fact worth quoting at dinner parties.

GMT / second time zone. A fourth hand geared 1 turn per 24 h; on "true" GMTs the keyless works can jump the local hour hand in one-hour steps without stopping the watch — a keyless-works trick, straight out of Chapter 07.

Power reserve indicator. A differential between the winding side and the running side of the barrel arbor: it literally displays the difference between energy in and energy out, pointing at a scale.

Tourbillon. The whole escapement and balance mounted in a rotating cage (usually 1 rev/min) so positional errors in the vertical positions average out — Chapter 12's problem, solved with 70+ extra parts. Glorious, and on the wrist, mostly ceremonial.

Repair triage for complications: they fail at their switching surfaces. Chronographs: worn clutch teeth or an eccentric out of adjustment (chrono hand stutters). Calendars: bent date finger or fatigued jumper spring from forced quicksets — always check whether the last owner set the date at midnight. Diagnose the base movement first; a sick going train makes every complication look broken.
Go deeper — perpetual calendars and minute repeaters

A perpetual calendar is mechanical memory. Its heart is a 48-month cam — a metal disc whose circumference encodes four years of month lengths, February's leap-year notch included, read once a day by a feeler lever that decides how far to advance the date. Program, read head, output: a mechanical ROM from 1795, drift-free until 2100 (a century year the Gregorian rule skips, and the cam doesn't know). Repair reality: perpetuals fail from owners, not wear — quicksetting during the changeover window shears the feeler or the cam followers, which is why every perpetual manual repeats the danger-zone warning in bold.

The heart cam — how a chronograph forgets. Reset-to-zero is a mechanical impossibility solved with a heart-shaped cam on each chronograph wheel: press reset and a flat-faced hammer falls on the heart. Wherever the cam stopped, the hammer slides it downhill — both flanks descend to the same notch — and the hand arrives at zero from either direction, instantly. Try stopping it anywhere:

00.0 sstopped
The notch is machined so the hand's zero is exact; a chrono hand that resets slightly off zero means a loose hand tube or a worn hammer face — never bend the hand.

A minute repeater reads the time by touch. Snail-shaped cams for hours, quarters and minutes rotate with the motion works; when the slide is pressed, spring-loaded racks fall onto the snails and their travel — how far each rack falls — is the time, converted to hammer strikes on tuned gongs curled around the movement. It is the motion works of Chapter 08 turned into a sensor, and the most unforgiving assembly in horology: a repairer's error doesn't lose seconds, it strikes the wrong hour out loud.
